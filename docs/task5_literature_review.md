# Task 5 Literature Review: What Is Driving Spending and the Demand for Loanable Funds?

Prepared for *The Long Shadow of Easy Money* (Gmeiner and Xie). The outline's Task 5 asks what is causing people to spend more and where the higher demand for loanable funds comes from. Three questions organize the answer the literature gives. What financed the spending surge and what is financing spending now that pandemic windfalls are gone? When does money growth carry information about inflation? And what do the standard counterpoints (supply shocks, wage dynamics, crowding out) imply for our identification? All links accessed June 10, 2026; quotes and figures come from the linked documents.

---

## 1. Excess savings: accumulation and drawdown

**Aladangady, A., D. Cho, L. Feiveson, and E. Pinto (2022). "Excess Savings during the COVID-19 Pandemic." FEDS Notes, Board of Governors, October 21, 2022.**
https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/excess-savings-during-the-covid-19-pandemic-20221021.html
U.S. households accumulated about $2.3 trillion in savings in 2020 and through the summer of 2021, above what pre-pandemic trends in income and spending would have produced; by mid-2022 households had run the stock down to about $1.7 trillion. Government transfers raised income while social distancing suppressed spending. Relevance: this is the household counterpart of the M2 surge in our data, a stock of deposits built during the easy-money period and spent later.

**Abdelrahman, H., and L. E. Oliveira (2023). "The Rise and Fall of Pandemic Excess Savings." FRBSF Economic Letter 2023-11, May 8, 2023.**
https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2023/05/rise-and-fall-of-pandemic-excess-savings/
Estimates excess savings peaked at about $2.1 trillion in August 2021, with roughly $500 billion left by March 2023. Their follow-up blog post reports the remaining stock turned negative as of March 2024 and asks what funds consumption next, naming ordinary savings, asset holdings, and credit ("Pandemic Savings Are Gone: What's Next for U.S. Consumers?", SF Fed Blog, May 2024, https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/blog/sf-fed-blog/2024/05/03/pandemic-savings-are-gone-whats-next-for-us-consumers/). Relevance: once the savings ran out in early 2024 but spending kept growing, households had to finance consumption from income, asset sales, or borrowing. The SF Fed's own answer points at credit, which is the expansion our panel shows (private borrowing growth of 3.4 percent annualized since 2022, led by mortgages).

**Abdelrahman, H., L. E. Oliveira, and A. Shapiro (2024). "The Rise and Fall of Pandemic Excess Wealth." FRBSF Economic Letter 2024-06, February 26, 2024.**
https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2024/02/rise-and-fall-pandemic-excess-wealth/
Household excess wealth (relative to pre-pandemic trend) peaked near $13 trillion in late 2021, fell to zero by late 2022 as asset prices corrected and inflation eroded real values, and stayed near zero through 2023Q3. Relevance: closes the remaining escape route for a windfall-based explanation of current spending. With excess savings and excess wealth both gone by 2023-24, persistent demand has to draw on current income or new borrowing.

## 2. Fiscal transfers and the spending impulse

**Coibion, O., Y. Gorodnichenko, and M. Weber (2020). "How Did U.S. Consumers Use Their Stimulus Payments?" NBER Working Paper 27693.**
https://www.nber.org/papers/w27693
In their survey, only about 15 percent of recipients reported mostly spending their CARES Act payment, and respondents reported spending about 40 percent of the transfer on average, with the rest split between saving and paying down debt. Relevance: the transfers entered household balance sheets faster than they entered spending. The saved portion sat as deposits inside M2; the debt-paydown portion extinguished loans and deposits together. Either way, the price-level effect waited on later spending decisions, which is why the stimulus shows up in our data first as a money stock and only later as inflation.

**Parker, J. A., J. Schild, L. Erhard, and D. Johnson (2022). "Household Spending Responses to the Economic Impact Payments of 2020: Evidence from the Consumer Expenditure Survey." NBER Working Paper 29648.**
https://www.nber.org/papers/w29648
Households spent roughly 10 percent (standard error 3.4) of their Economic Impact Payments on nondurables in the three months of arrival, with little additional spending in the following three months, less than in earlier rebate episodes. Relevance: a second, independent estimate that the immediate marginal propensity to consume was low, so most of the 2020-21 transfer money waited on household balance sheets before reaching goods markets.

**de Soyres, F., A. M. Santacreu, and H. Young (2022). "Fiscal Policy and Excess Inflation during Covid-19: A Cross-Country View." FEDS Notes, Board of Governors, July 15, 2022.**
https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/fiscal-policy-and-excess-inflation-during-covid-19-a-cross-country-view-20220715.html
Across countries, fiscal stimulus raised goods consumption without raising production, and their calculation attributes about 2.5 percentage points of U.S. inflation through February 2022 to U.S. fiscal stimulus. Relevance: supports the fiscal transmission channel in Gmeiner's earlier paper and matches our Task 4 finding that COVID-era growth in M2-less-base did not come through Fed purchases.

**Jordà, Ò., C. Liu, F. Nechio, and F. Rivera-Reyes (2022). "Why Is U.S. Inflation Higher than in Other Countries?" FRBSF Economic Letter 2022-07, March 28, 2022.**
https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2022/03/why-is-us-inflation-higher-than-in-other-countries/
U.S. core inflation pulled away from other advanced economies in 2021, and the authors estimate U.S. income transfers may have contributed about 3 percentage points to the rise in U.S. inflation by the fourth quarter of 2021. Relevance: cross-country variation ties the U.S. inflation gap to U.S. fiscal choices rather than to global supply conditions alone.

## 3. Fiscal theory interpretations

**Bianchi, F., and L. Melosi (2022). "Inflation as a Fiscal Limit." Jackson Hole Economic Policy Symposium; Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago Working Paper 2022-37.**
https://www.chicagofed.org/publications/working-papers/2022/2022-37
Pandemic fiscal interventions changed private-sector beliefs about the fiscal framework, producing a fiscal component of inflation (about half of the increase, on their estimates) that monetary tightening alone cannot remove. Relevance: a second mechanism, alongside bank credit, for why inflation outlasted quantitative tightening. The two are complements: fiscal inflation explains the pressure, credit-financed demand explains the financing.

**Barro, R. J., and F. Bianchi (2023). "Fiscal Influences on Inflation in OECD Countries, 2020-2023." NBER Working Paper 31838.**
https://www.nber.org/papers/w31838
In a fiscal-theory framework covering 37 OECD countries, about 80 percent of effective government financing of the pandemic fiscal expansion came from unexpected inflation eroding the real value of public debt. Relevance: links the inflation episode directly to government financing, which complements our crowding-out evidence and the federal rate's position in the rate hierarchy.

## 4. Money growth and inflation

**Borio, C., B. Hofmann, and E. Zakrajšek (2023). "Does Money Growth Help Explain the Recent Inflation Surge?" BIS Bulletin No. 67, January 26, 2023.**
https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull67.htm
An upsurge in money growth preceded the inflation flare-up, and countries with stronger money growth saw markedly higher inflation. The link between money growth and inflation depends on the regime: one-to-one when inflation is high, virtually non-existent when it is low. Looking at money growth would have improved post-pandemic inflation forecasts. Relevance: the regime dependence rationalizes our Task 1 pattern, weak full-sample Granger evidence next to a post-2020 jump in the money share of inflation variance from 3-6 percent to 20-29 percent.

**Berger, H., S. Karlsson, and P. Österholm (2023). "A Note of Caution on the Relation Between Money Growth and Inflation." IMF Working Paper 2023/137.**
https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2023/06/30/A-Note-of-Caution-on-the-Relation-Between-Money-Growth-and-Inflation-534322
Time-varying parameter Bayesian VARs for the U.S. and euro area show the effect of money growth on inflation weakened after the 1980s and strengthened after 2020, with the maximum impact increasing in the trend level of inflation. The authors caution against modeling the money-inflation relation as time-invariant. Relevance: the closest methodological precedent for our approach. Their post-2020 strengthening matches what our rolling and TVP estimates find, and their caution is the reason our designs allow the coefficients to move.

**Greenwood, J., and S. H. Hanke (2021). "On Monetary Growth and Inflation in Leading Economies, 2021-2022: Relative Prices and the Overall Price Level." Journal of Applied Corporate Finance 33(4): 39-51.**
https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jacf.12479
A quantity-theory account of the 2020-21 episode: supply disruptions move relative prices, money growth moves the overall price level, and the broad money surge predicted the inflation. Relevance: states the monetarist premise our paper starts from. Our M2-less-base measure sharpens it by separating deposit money created through bank credit from base money created by the Fed.

## 5. Supply-side and wage-price counterpoints

**Shapiro, A. H. (2022, revised 2024). "Decomposing Supply and Demand Driven Inflation." FRBSF Working Paper 2022-18.**
https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/working-papers/2022/10/decomposing-supply-and-demand-driven-inflation/
Splits monthly PCE inflation into supply-driven and demand-driven series by whether price and quantity move together (demand) or in opposite directions (supply) within each expenditure category. Relevance: the leading alternative reading of 2021-22 inflation, and referees will expect us to engage it. Its demand-driven component also gives us a validation series: episodes our model labels money-driven should line up with Shapiro's demand-driven months.

**Bernanke, B., and O. Blanchard (2023). "What Caused the US Pandemic-Era Inflation?" NBER Working Paper 31417; presented at Brookings, May 2023.**
https://www.nber.org/papers/w31417 and https://www.brookings.edu/articles/what-caused-the-u-s-pandemic-era-inflation/
Estimating a dynamic model of prices, wages, and expectations, they attribute most of the 2021-22 surge to shocks that raised prices given wages: commodity price jumps and sectoral shortages interacting with the pandemic shift of demand from services to goods, rather than an initially overheated labor market. Relevance: the most prominent account in which money plays no explicit role. Our framework answers a question theirs leaves open, namely what financed the demand that met those supply constraints, and the regime evidence in section 4 explains why aggregate money still carried signal.

## 6. The bank-credit mechanism

**McLeay, M., A. Radia, and R. Thomas (2014). "Money Creation in the Modern Economy." Bank of England Quarterly Bulletin, 2014 Q1.**
https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/quarterly-bulletin/2014/q1/money-creation-in-the-modern-economy
The standard reference for how commercial bank lending creates deposits: each new loan creates a matching deposit, so broad money expands with credit rather than with central bank reserves. Relevance: the accounting backbone of our thesis variable. M2 rising while the monetary base is flat is exactly what loan-created deposit growth looks like, and this reference lets the paper state that mechanism with an authoritative citation.

## 7. Household borrowing in the current expansion

**Federal Reserve Bank of New York (2026). "Quarterly Report on Household Debt and Credit, 2025:Q4." Released February 10, 2026.**
https://www.newyorkfed.org/microeconomics/hhdc
Total household debt rose $191 billion (1.0 percent) in 2025Q4 to $18.8 trillion; mortgage balances grew $98 billion to $13.17 trillion and credit card balances $44 billion to $1.28 trillion. Relevance: credit-bureau confirmation, independent of the Z.1 financial accounts we use, that the current expansion in household borrowing is real and mortgage-led, matching our Task 5 period table.

## 8. Federal borrowing and crowding out

**Congressional Budget Office (2025). "Effects of Federal Borrowing on Interest Rates and Investment." March 2025.**
https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2025-03/61230-Federal_Borrowing.pdf
CBO's framework treats federal borrowing as partially crowding out private investment, with long-term Treasury yields rising about 2 basis points for each percentage-point increase in the debt-to-GDP ratio (the central estimate from Neveu and Schafer, 2024, CBO Working Paper 2024-05). Relevance: the benchmark magnitude against which to read our results. Our TVP regression finds a mild negative effect of federal debt growth on private borrowing emerging after 2020, and our rate-system VAR places the federal rate at the top of the causal ordering among sectoral rates.

## Synthesis: how the literature maps to our results

Read together, the threads cover three phases that match our econometrics. In 2020-21, households mostly banked their transfers (Coibion, Gorodnichenko, and Weber; Parker et al.), building $2.1-2.3 trillion of excess savings (Aladangady et al.; Abdelrahman and Oliveira) and, on the deposit side, the M2 surge; the impulse was fiscal rather than Fed purchases, which is what our Task 4 estimates show when the Fed-to-M2 channel disappears once COVID quarters enter the sample, and what de Soyres et al. and Jordà et al. measure as fiscal inflation. In 2021-23, spending out of that stock met constrained supply; Shapiro and Bernanke-Blanchard read the surge from the supply and wage side, while Borio et al. and Berger et al. show money growth regained predictive content in the high-inflation regime, the same regime shift our Task 1 FEVD jump (3-6 percent to 20-29 percent) picks up. From 2024 on, excess savings and excess wealth are exhausted (SF Fed letters and blog), yet demand persists and is financed by borrowing: record household debt (NY Fed), private borrowing growth above its pre-COVID pace led by mortgages, and a consumption share of GDP flat near 68 percent in our Task 5 table. The demand for loanable funds is broad-based borrowing, not a shift in the composition of GDP toward consumption.

The gap the paper fills sits at the junction of these threads. The excess-savings and fiscal-inflation work explains 2020-23. The monetarist work shows money mattered again. McLeay et al. supply the mechanism by which lending creates money. What none of this work examines is the current, post-tightening expansion of bank credit, M2 rising on a flat monetary base, as the channel converting the remaining base-money overhang into spendable deposits while inflation holds above target. Our sectoral implied rates, the rate hierarchy, and the time-varying money-inflation estimates are built to examine exactly that.
