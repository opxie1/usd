# Task 5 Literature Review: What Is Driving Spending and the Demand for Loanable Funds?

Prepared for *The Long Shadow of Easy Money* (Gmeiner and Xie). Addresses the outline's Task 5 question: what does the literature say is causing people to spend more, and what does that imply about the source of higher demand for loanable funds? All links accessed June 10, 2026. Every citation below was verified against the linked source; numbers are taken from the cited documents, not from memory.

---

## 1. Excess savings: accumulation and drawdown

**Aladangady, A., D. Cho, L. Feiveson, and E. Pinto (2022). "Excess Savings during the COVID-19 Pandemic." FEDS Notes, Board of Governors, October 21, 2022.**
https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/excess-savings-during-the-covid-19-pandemic-20221021.html
Estimates U.S. households accumulated about $2.3 trillion in savings in 2020 through summer 2021 above the pre-pandemic trend, driven by historic government transfers raising income while social distancing suppressed spending. Relevance: quantifies the stock of latent purchasing power created during the easy-money period; this is the household-side counterpart of the monetary base overhang in our thesis.

**Abdelrahman, H., and L. E. Oliveira (2023). "The Rise and Fall of Pandemic Excess Savings." FRBSF Economic Letter 2023-11, May 2023.**
https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2023/05/rise-and-fall-of-pandemic-excess-savings/
Estimates roughly $2.1 trillion in accumulated excess savings, with about $500 billion remaining by March 2023. The San Francisco Fed's follow-up blog series estimated depletion during 2023Q3 and reported that remaining aggregate excess savings turned negative by March 2024 ("Pandemic Savings Are Gone: What's Next for U.S. Consumers?", SF Fed Blog, May 2024, https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/blog/sf-fed-blog/2024/05/03/pandemic-savings-are-gone-whats-next-for-us-consumers/). Relevance: if excess savings were exhausted by early 2024 but spending kept growing, continued consumption has to be financed out of current income, asset drawdowns, or borrowing. That pivot to borrowing is exactly the credit expansion our paper documents (private borrowing growth of 3.4 percent annualized since 2022, led by mortgages).

**Abdelrahman, H., L. E. Oliveira, and A. Shapiro (2024). "The Rise and Fall of Pandemic Excess Wealth." FRBSF Economic Letter, February 2024.**
https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2024/02/rise-and-fall-pandemic-excess-wealth/
Extends the savings accounting to household wealth. Relevance: housing and equity wealth gains from the easy-money period support borrowing capacity (collateral) even after liquid excess savings are gone.

## 2. Fiscal transfers and the spending impulse

**Coibion, O., Y. Gorodnichenko, and M. Weber (2020). "How Did U.S. Consumers Use Their Stimulus Payments?" NBER Working Paper 27693.**
https://www.nber.org/papers/w27693
Survey evidence on the CARES Act payments (up to $1,200 per adult): only about 15 percent of recipients reported mostly spending the transfer, and respondents reported spending roughly 40 percent of the total on average, with the rest saved or used to pay down debt. Relevance: the initial transfers were largely stored as deposits (M2), not spent immediately, which is how stimulus became a stock of latent demand rather than an instant price-level jump.

**Parker, J. A., J. Schild, L. Erhard, and D. Johnson (2022). "Household Spending Responses to the Economic Impact Payments of 2020: Evidence from the Consumer Expenditure Survey." NBER Working Paper 29648.**
https://www.nber.org/papers/w29648
Using the CE Survey, estimates households spent roughly 10 percent (s.e. 3.4) of their Economic Impact Payments on nondurables within three months of receipt, lower than in previous rebate episodes. Relevance: corroborates the delayed-spending mechanism with administrative-quality survey data; the marginal propensity to consume out of the 2020 transfers was unusually low at first, consistent with pandemic constraints.

**de Soyres, F., A. M. Santacreu, and H. Young (2022). "Fiscal Policy and Excess Inflation during Covid-19: A Cross-Country View." FEDS Notes, Board of Governors, July 15, 2022.**
https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/fiscal-policy-and-excess-inflation-during-covid-19-a-cross-country-view-20220715.html
Cross-country evidence that fiscal stimulus raised goods consumption without raising production, creating excess demand. Their calculation attributes about 2.5 percentage points of U.S. inflation through February 2022 to U.S. fiscal stimulus. Relevance: direct empirical support for the fiscal transmission channel in Gmeiner's earlier work and for our Task 4 finding that COVID-era M2 growth was not Fed-purchase-driven.

**Jordà, Ò., C. Liu, F. Nechio, and F. Rivera-Reyes (2022). "Why Is U.S. Inflation Higher than in Other Countries?" FRBSF Economic Letter 2022-07, March 2022.**
https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2022/03/why-is-us-inflation-higher-than-in-other-countries/
U.S. inflation outpaced other advanced economies beginning in 2021; pandemic fiscal support measures may account for about 3 percentage points of the difference by end-2021. Relevance: cross-country identification that demand-side fiscal support, not just global supply shocks, drove the U.S. inflation surge.

## 3. Fiscal theory interpretations

**Bianchi, F., and L. Melosi (2022). "Inflation as a Fiscal Limit." Jackson Hole Economic Policy Symposium; Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago Working Paper 2022-37.**
https://www.chicagofed.org/publications/working-papers/2022/2022-37
Argues pandemic fiscal interventions changed beliefs about the fiscal framework, generating a fiscal component of inflation (roughly half of the increase) that monetary tightening alone cannot remove. Relevance: explains why inflation persisted through quantitative tightening, the puzzle our paper addresses through the bank-credit channel; fiscal inflation and credit-financed demand are complementary explanations.

**Barro, R. J., and F. Bianchi (2023). "Fiscal Influences on Inflation in OECD Countries, 2020-2023." NBER Working Paper 31838.**
https://www.nber.org/papers/w31838
Applies the fiscal theory of the price level to 37 OECD countries; estimates imply about 80 percent of effective pandemic financing came from unexpected inflation eroding the real value of public debt. Relevance: ties the inflation episode to government financing needs, complementing our federal-debt and crowding-out results.

## 4. Money growth and inflation

**Borio, C., B. Hofmann, and E. Zakrajšek (2023). "Does Money Growth Help Explain the Recent Inflation Surge?" BIS Bulletin No. 67, January 2023.**
https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull67.htm
An upsurge in money growth preceded the inflation flare-up, and countries with stronger money growth had higher inflation. The money-inflation link is regime-dependent: close to one-to-one in high-inflation regimes and essentially absent in low-inflation regimes; money growth would have improved post-pandemic inflation forecasts. Relevance: directly rationalizes our Task 1 finding that full-sample Granger evidence is weak while the post-2020 money share of inflation variance jumps from 3-6 percent to 20-29 percent.

**Berger, H., S. Karlsson, and P. Österholm (2023). "A Note of Caution on the Relation Between Money Growth and Inflation." IMF Working Paper 2023/137.**
https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2023/06/30/A-Note-of-Caution-on-the-Relation-Between-Money-Growth-and-Inflation-534322
Time-varying parameter Bayesian VARs for the U.S. and euro area show the money-to-inflation effect is statistically unstable: it weakened after the 1980s and strengthened after 2020, with the maximum impact increasing in trend inflation. Relevance: the closest methodological precedent for our time-varying approach, and independent confirmation of the post-2020 strengthening we estimate; also a caution against asserting a time-invariant money-inflation relation, which our rolling and TVP designs respect.

**Greenwood, J., and S. H. Hanke (2021). "On Monetary Growth and Inflation in Leading Economies, 2021-2022: Relative Prices and the Overall Price Level." Journal of Applied Corporate Finance 33(4): 39-51.**
https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jacf.12479
Quantity-theory argument that the 2020-21 broad money surge, not supply-chain disruption alone, determined the path of the overall price level; supply problems change relative prices, money determines the price level. Relevance: the strongest monetarist statement of our paper's premise; our M2-less-base measure refines it by separating bank-credit money from base money.

## 5. The supply-side counterpoint

**Shapiro, A. H. (2022, published 2026). "Decomposing Supply- and Demand-Driven Inflation." FRBSF Working Paper 2022-18; Journal of Money, Credit and Banking.**
https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/working-papers/2022/10/decomposing-supply-and-demand-driven-inflation/
Classifies PCE categories monthly by whether price and quantity surprises co-move (demand) or move oppositely (supply); both components contributed to 2021-22 inflation, and monetary tightening reduces the demand-driven component. The San Francisco Fed maintains the decomposition as a public data series. Relevance: the leading alternative to money- and fiscal-centric stories; any referee will expect us to engage it. Useful empirically because its demand-driven component is a candidate validation series for our money-driven inflation episodes.

## 6. Household borrowing in the current expansion

**Federal Reserve Bank of New York (2026). "Quarterly Report on Household Debt and Credit, 2025:Q4." Released February 10, 2026.**
https://www.newyorkfed.org/microeconomics/hhdc and https://www.newyorkfed.org/newsevents/news/research/2026/20260210
Total household debt rose $191 billion (1.0 percent) in 2025Q4 to $18.8 trillion, with mortgage balances at $13.17 trillion and credit card balances at $1.28 trillion; 4.8 percent of outstanding debt was in some stage of delinquency. Relevance: independent loan-level confirmation of the borrowing expansion our Z.1-based panel shows (private borrowing growth above its pre-COVID pace, led by mortgages), sourced from credit bureau data rather than financial accounts.

## 7. Federal borrowing and crowding out

**Congressional Budget Office (2025). "Effects of Federal Borrowing on Interest Rates and Investment." March 2025.**
https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2025-03/61230-Federal_Borrowing.pdf
CBO's standard elasticity: each percentage-point increase in the debt-to-GDP ratio raises real 10-year rates by about 2 basis points, with federal borrowing partially crowding out private capital (CBO working papers, including Gamber and Seliski 2019, use a crowding-out parameter of about 0.6, with foreign capital inflows offsetting part of the effect; see also https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2024-12/60314-DSIR-WP.pdf). Relevance: benchmark magnitudes for our crowding-out evidence; our TVP regression finds a mild negative effect of federal debt growth on private borrowing emerging after 2020, and our Tasks 2-3 system locates the federal rate at the top of the rate hierarchy.

## Synthesis: how the literature maps to our results

The literature supports a three-act narrative that matches our econometrics. Act one, 2020-21: fiscal transfers (Coibion et al.; Parker et al.) were initially saved, building roughly $2.1-2.3 trillion of excess savings (Aladangady et al.; Abdelrahman and Oliveira) and a surge in M2; the fiscal impulse, not Fed purchases per se, drove broad money, which is exactly our Task 4 finding that the Fed-to-M2 channel disappears once COVID quarters enter the sample (consistent with Gmeiner's fiscal transmission paper, and with de Soyres et al. and Jordà et al. on fiscal inflation). Act two, 2021-23: spending out of the savings stock met constrained supply and inflation surged, with both demand and supply components (Shapiro) and a regime shift that made money growth informative again (Borio et al.; Berger et al., whose post-2020 strengthening of the money-inflation link is the same pattern as our Task 1 FEVD jump from 3-6 to 20-29 percent). Act three, 2024-present: excess savings are exhausted (SF Fed), yet demand persists, financed increasingly by borrowing; household debt reaches record levels (NY Fed), private borrowing growth runs above its pre-COVID pace led by mortgages (our Task 5 table), and consumption's GDP share stays flat at about 68 percent, telling us the loanable-funds demand is broad-based borrowing rather than a consumption-share shift.

The gap our paper fills: the excess-savings and fiscal-inflation literatures explain acts one and two, and the monetarist literature documents that money mattered again, but little of this work examines the current, post-tightening expansion of bank credit (M2 rising while the monetary base is flat) as the mechanism converting leftover base money into spendable money now. Our combination of NIPA-implied sectoral rates, the rate hierarchy, and the time-varying money-inflation link is aimed at that gap.
