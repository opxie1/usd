\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

This section describes the data, the variables we build from it, and the plots we use in place of summary statistics. We downloaded all of the series from the Federal Reserve Bank of St.\ Louis (FRED) database. Table~\ref{tab:variables} lists each series with its units, source agency, and coverage. The panel is quarterly and runs from 1959 through 2026 for the series that reach back that far. When a series is reported at a higher frequency, we convert it to quarterly. We average interest rates and price indexes within the quarter, and we take end-of-quarter values for stocks of money and debt. The Financial Accounts (Z.1) and national accounts (NIPA) data are already quarterly.

We build three groups of variables from the raw data. The money measure is M2 minus the monetary base, in billions of dollars. Inflation is the annualized quarterly log difference of each price index. Where we use the year-over-year change instead, we say so. The sectoral interest rates need a longer explanation because they are unconventional. We compute them as interest payments divided by the stock of debt in each sector, which gives an average rate on everything the sector owes. For the state and local rate we subtract the imputed interest on public pension plans that NIPA counts as interest payments, since it is not a borrowing cost. For mortgages we use the market thirty-year fixed rate instead, because mortgages last for decades and the average rate on old loans says little about the cost of a new one. We also collect market rates for each sector and use them later as a robustness check.

From there, because this is a time-series study, we present the data as time series plots instead of conventional summary statistics. Figures~\ref{fig:money} through~\ref{fig:fed} plot the money supply, inflation, debt by sector, the five interest rates, and the Federal Reserve balance sheet. Figure~\ref{fig:impliedvsmarket} compares our implied rates with the market rates sector by sector.

\section{Money Supply and Inflation}
\label{sec:money_inflation}

We begin with the link between money growth and inflation. The model is a vector autoregression (VAR) in three variables: real output growth, the growth of M2 less the monetary base, and inflation. Each equation regresses one of the variables on past values of all three. Before estimating anything we test for unit roots. The augmented Dickey--Fuller and KPSS tests (Table~\ref{tab:unitroots}) agree that the log levels are nonstationary and that growth rates and inflation can be treated as stationary, so the VAR is in growth rates. We choose the lag length by minimizing the Akaike criterion, up to eight lags, and we check that each estimated VAR is stable. We then run Granger-causality tests and compute impulse responses with a Cholesky ordering that puts output first and prices last.

Table~\ref{tab:task1} collects the results for the four inflation measures. Over the full sample, the evidence that money growth Granger-causes inflation is weak. Before the pandemic, the stronger direction runs the other way, from inflation to money. After 2020 the share of inflation variance explained by money shocks is larger, but twenty-five quarters is too short a sample for any precision, so we do not put much weight on it. Figure~\ref{fig:task1irf} plots the response of inflation to a money-growth shock. Figure~\ref{fig:rollgranger} repeats the Granger test in a rolling window and shows how the relationship has moved over time.

\section{The Interest-Rate System}
\label{sec:rates}

Next we ask how the five sectoral rates respond to the money supply and to each other. To pick the right specification, we first test for cointegration with Johansen trace tests in levels and in differences (Table~\ref{tab:johansen}). The implied rates are not cointegrated in levels, so we model them as a VAR in first differences (Table~\ref{tab:ratehier_implied}). The market rates are cointegrated, and differencing them would erase their long-run relationships, so we model them with a vector error-correction model (VECM) instead (Table~\ref{tab:ratehier_market}). Interest rates are persistent, so we also confirm the causality results with lag-augmented (Toda--Yamamoto) tests, which stay valid when variables are close to unit roots.

In the market-rate VECM the federal rate Granger-causes the other four rates, and the reverse direction is insignificant. The hierarchy runs one way, consistent with the federal rate leading the rest of the system. In the differenced implied-rate system the federal rate still leads, but some feedback from the other rates shows up. Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} maps the full set of pairwise tests. The cointegration results also tell a story of their own. The market rates share a long-run trend, but the rates borrowers pay in each sector do not. This suggests borrower behavior differs across sectors.

\section{Federal Reserve Transactions}
\label{sec:fed}

To measure the central bank's role directly, we estimate a six-variable system starting in 2003, since the balance-sheet series begin in late 2002. The variables are the growth of Federal Reserve securities holdings, base-money growth, the growth of M2 less base, the change in the federal funds rate, the change in the ten-year yield, and CPI inflation.

Table~\ref{tab:task4granger} reports the Granger tests. Federal Reserve purchases drive the monetary base in both samples. Before the pandemic they also drive broad money and the funds rate. Once the pandemic quarters are added, the link from purchases to broad money weakens. That fits the view that the pandemic rise in M2 came through fiscal programs instead of open-market operations. The Johansen tests find cointegration in this system, so we fit a VECM as well. Table~\ref{tab:task4vecm} reports it two ways, with a constant only and with a constant and a trend, along with residual checks. Figure~\ref{fig:task4irf} plots the responses to a shock to securities purchases.

\section{The Demand for Loanable Funds}
\label{sec:loanable}

Finally we ask where the higher demand for loanable funds comes from. Table~\ref{tab:task5period} compares average growth rates before, during, and after the pandemic. Figure~\ref{fig:shares} plots the components of GDP as shares of the total. Consumption's share has stayed close to flat over the whole period. The extra borrowing is spread across sectors and does not reflect a shift toward consumption.

To see how the relationships change over time, we estimate regressions with time-varying coefficients. For each sector we regress borrowing growth on the sector's own interest rate and on lagged money growth. We estimate each equation two ways, with a Kalman smoother that lets the coefficients follow random walks and with a rolling least-squares window, and we repeat everything with both money measures. Table~\ref{tab:task5b} reports the coefficient on money growth by sector, and Figure~\ref{fig:task5b} plots it over time. Consumer borrowing carries a positive coefficient on money growth. Business borrowing does not, and after 2020 its coefficient turns negative. Business borrowing is the kind that should be matched by new production, so it makes sense that the money-growth effect is weakest there.

\section{Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}

We check the main results in five ways: 1) alternative orderings for the impulse responses, 2) alternative forms for inflation, 3) a test for a structural break, 4) a check on whether the time-varying coefficients move at all, and 5) a list of which headline results are fragile. Table~\ref{tab:ordering} recomputes the variance decompositions under different recursive orderings, and the shares barely move. Table~\ref{tab:inflform} repeats the money-inflation tests with inflation in levels and in first differences, and the conclusions hold in both forms. Table~\ref{tab:break} reports a Quandt--Andrews test for a structural break with a bootstrap $p$-value. The main break in the money-inflation relation lands in 1981 for both inflation measures, not in 2020, so we treat the post-2020 evidence as descriptive. Table~\ref{tab:constancy} asks whether the time-varying coefficients vary at all. Several turn out close to constant, while the business coefficient on money growth does move. Table~\ref{tab:fragility} lists which of the headline money-inflation results are fragile, with $p$-values between 0.05 and 0.10.
